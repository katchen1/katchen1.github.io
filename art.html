Artist's Statement

To me, art is autobiographical; it blends my feelings, visions, and intellectual interests into expressions shared with my viewers. I am particularly intrigued by sculptures due to the breath of materials I can play with, from Styrofoam and clay to paper cups and wires. My sculptures have been influenced by contemporary artists such as Tony Cragg. For example, his public installation Points of View, composed of stacked and twisted horizontal layers, inspired me to craft my sculptures Sprint, Infinite Loop, and Violin Variations with a sense of movement. By probing into novel techniques, merging media boundaries, and integrating my multifaceted thoughts, I strive to render artwork that grants me a voice to deliver my perspectives to the world, as if I am composing my own rhapsody.

Selected Artwork

AI Melody

Date: May 2018

Materials: Styrofoam, Paper Mask, Paper Cups & Plates, Recycled Electronic Devices, Acrylic Paint, Wood Board

Dimensions: W 33.1" x D 26.4" x H 19.5"

An era of artificial intelligence is impending, and I am excited to see how autonomous devices can enhance our experiences in daily life. By extracting components from recycled electronic devices, for example, antennas and curcuit boards from telephones and keyboards, and rearranging them to form a futuristic person playing a violin, I juxtaposed the technicality of electric wares and the expressiveness of music to reveal my imagination and hopes for the future.

Evolution of the Dragon with 99 Chinese Calligraphic Characters

Date: December 2016

Materials: Plexiglass, Wood, Light Tube

Dimensions: W 39.0" x D 9.0" x H 9.0"

Born at the turn of the millenium, in the year of the Dragon, I've always had a deep connection with the idea of the Chinese dragon, which stemmed from animalistic beliefs thousands of years ago. This sculpture celebrates the history of the calligraphic Dragon and illustrates a cross of my interests in technology, math, calligraphy, and art. I extracted 99 forms of the Chinese calligraphic character of Dragon, imprinting them onto acrylic boards using a laser cutter. The gradient heights of the boards were calculated with parabolic and sine functions. Arranging the characters in chronological order from the oldest forms to the ones we see today, I portrayed the evolution of the Dragon. Utilizing the transparency of acrylic boards, I installed a light tube in the base. The light refracted and outlined the edges of the characters, giving the Dragon a mythic glow.

Infinite Loop

Date: January 2018

Materials: Balloon, Newspaper, Paper Mask, Wood Veneers, Wood Disc, Acrylic Paint

Dimensions: W 18.5" x D 13.8" x H 17.7"

In computer science, infinite loops are often undesired as they prevent programs from reaching their ends. This three-dimensional portrait of me has wood veneers looped around a head and is a metaphor for problems I encounter both in coding and in life. The whirl extending above represents my continuous discoveries and attempts to break free from the infinite loop.

Mirroring: Zero & One Self-Portrait

Date: May 2017

Materials: Styrofoam, Acrylic Paint, Spray Paint

Dimensions: W 16.0" x D 11.0" x H 29.0"

Zeros and ones are the binary digits, the building blocks of computer software, a subject of my interest. In this Styrofoam sculpture, I mirrored my upper body vertically, transformed it into zeros and ones, and arranged them to form the reflection of my head, similar to a flower blossoming with ideas. At the back of the sculpture, I carved holes shaped like stairs leading up to doors. These voids were inspired by the staircases in Egyptian pyramids, and they symbolize secret paths that lead to my inner thoughts.

Sprint

Date: July 2017

Materials: Pencils, Wood Block, Acrylic Rod

Dimensions: W 13.2" x D 6.5" x H 12.4"

People often try to overcome obstacles in life, which I interpret as one sprinting hard. I chose to visualize my interpretation using pencils, a material that I have been using since childhood. I cut pencils in different lengths and stacked them to form an athlete sprinting with speed and fluidity. In doing so, I encourage everyone to sprint with energy to conquer barriers in their lives.

Violin Variations

Date: August 2017

Materials: Cardboard, Violin Bow, Sheet Music, Acrylic Paint, Plexiglass

Dimensions: W 26.6" x D 13.0" x H 25.6"

In this sculpture, I hope to express my fondness for music and my violin, which has accompanied me through nearly a decade of practiceing the instrument. Using cardboard, I put together a violin in a one to one ratio, and divided it into segments that appear to be floating and dancing to a melody. The irregular shape of the violin imbues it with a sense of movement. Finally, I covered the violin's surface with sheet music in order to further emphasize its musicality.

Chinese Calligraphy 2016

Date: August 2016

Materials: Xuan Paper, Chinese Ink

Dimensions: W 27.0" x H 53.0" paper; W 7.5" x H 5.0" (300 dpi) digital image

In this Chinese calligraphy piece, I wrote a poem in the running script. The smaller characters towards the left are the trainling ones of the poem and were written with a smaller ink brush. I also experimented with its digital presentation by Photoshopping a zoomed-in, inverted, and grayscale background of the original work.

Chinese Calligraphy 2017

Date: August 2017

Materials: Xuan Paper, Chinese Ink

Dimensions: W 27.0" x H 53.0" paper; W 5.4" x H 7.0" (300 dpi) digital image

This is a work of Chinese calligraphy, the art of writing Chinese characters. With a brush and ink on Xuan paper, I wrote two poems composed by one of my favorite poets, Bai Juyi, describing his reunion with a friend and the beautiful landscapes of his hometown. Focusing on the aesthetics of calligraphy, I wanted to show my dedication to mastering this art and acknowledge my ties to Chinese traditions.

Landscape Rhythms

Date: April 2017

Materials: Chinese Ink, Xuan Paper, Transparent Film, White-Out, Acrylic Paint

Dimensions: W 31.5" x D 1.4" x H 30.3

In this work, I portrayed traditional Chinese landscape paintings in new ways. First, I reversed one of my calligraphic works, printed it onto a transparent film, and attached it to the back of two of my Chinese ink paintings. Then, I cut out shapes of rocks. To create a sense of depth, I arranged them on three transparent films and drew elements of Chinese landscape paintings—mountains and a waterfall—rendering a multi-layer landscape with the cutouts, white-out, and acrylic paint. Shining light through the work, I let the calligraphy transcend above the earth and become visible from the front. The rhythmic Chinese verses, read from top to bottom, echo the flowing waterfall. This work, as a result, links the past to the present, acknowledging both my cultural traditions and contemporary mixed-media techniques.
