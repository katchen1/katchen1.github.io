Extending minBERT for Sentence-Level Tasks

NLP with Deep Learning (CS 224N) Winter 2023

Abstract: Pre-trained models such as BERT have proven to be effective in various natural language processing tasks, but fine-tuning these models for specific downstream tasks remains challenging. In this project, we aim to improve BERT's performance on downstream tasks, namely sentiment analysis, paraphrase detection, and semantic textual similarity. We do this by implementing contrastive learning and regularization methods, namely regularized optimization, and varying dropout probabilities and learning rates. We also compare the performance of different fine-tuning model architectures as well as the effects of multi-task and single-task fine-tuning. We evaluate our model using three datasets, and use minBERT with pre-trained weights and no modifications as our baseline. Our approach moderately improves the performance of minBERT on all datasets. However, our main finding is that the best performance was obtained with a linear architecture that did not use contrastive learning, regularization methods or multi-task fine-tuning. This demonstrates that the effectiveness of augmentations to fine-tuning may be highly sensitive to dataset and architecture.

Strategy Optimization in Choice Poker

Decision Making Under Uncertainty (CS 238) Fall 2021

Abstract: In this project, we explore online belief state planning methods for optimizing actions in Choice Poker, a poker variation from the Kakegurui anime series. We focus on online planning because Choice Poker has a large and computationally intractable state space. We first model Choice Poker as a partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP) that can be split into two independent smaller POMDPs, then implement Monte Carlo tree search to solve the sub-POMDPs while experimenting with different opponent policies and reward metrics. We use a random policy as a baseline to evaluate the performance of our algorithms.
